1. We find that \(A\vvec_1=\twovec{2.6}{1.3} = 1.3\vvec_1\) and \(A\vvec_2=\twovec{-1}{0.5}=0.5\vvec_2\text{.}\) This shows that \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\) are eigenvectors with associated eigenvalues \(\lambda_1=1.3\) and \(\lambda_2=0.5\text{.}\)
  2. Solving for the weights of the linear combination of \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\) that produces \(\xvec_0\text{,}\) we find that \(\xvec_0=2\vvec_1+\vvec_2\text{.}\)
  3. Each time we multiply by \(A\text{,}\) the eigenvectors are multiplied by their associated eigenvalues. This gives
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \xvec_1 = A\xvec_0 \amp = 2(1.3)\vvec_1 + 0.5\vvec_2 \\ \xvec_2 = A\xvec_1 \amp = 2(1.3)^2\vvec_1 + 0.5^2\vvec_2 \\ \xvec_3 = A\xvec_2 \amp = 2(1.3)^3\vvec_1 + 0.5^3\vvec_2\text{.} \\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
  4. After a long time \(0.5^k\approx 0\) so we have \(\xvec_k \approx 2(1.3)^k\vvec_1\text{.}\)
  5. More generally, we have \(\xvec_k=2(1.3)^k\vvec_1 + 0.5^k\vvec_2\text{.}\) As \(k\) grows large, \(0.5^k\) becomes insignificantly small so that \(\xvec_k\approx 2(1.3)^k\vvec_1\text{.}\) This means that \(R_k\approx 4(1.3)^k\) and \(S_k\approx2(1.3)^k\) so that the ratio \(R_k/S_k \approx 2\text{.}\)
  6. We see that \(\xvec_{k+1}\approx 1.3\xvec_k\) so that the populations both grow by a factor of approximately 1.3, which is a 30% growth rate.
  7. Now we have \(\xvec_0 = 3\vvec_1 + \vvec_2\text{.}\) In the same way, when \(k \) is very large, we have \(\xvec_k\approx 3(1.3)^k\vvec_1\) so that \(R_k\approx6(1.3)^k\) and \(S_k\approx3(1.3)^k\text{.}\) This gives the same ratio: \(R_k/S_k\approx 2\text{.}\)

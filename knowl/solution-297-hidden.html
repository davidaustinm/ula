1. Since \(\vvec_1\text{,}\) \(\vvec_2\text{,}\) and \(\vvec_3\) form a basis for \(\real^3\text{,}\) any vector in \(\real^3\) can be written as a linear combination of them.
  2. \(\vvec\cdot\vvec_1 = c_1\vvec_1\cdot\vvec_1\) so that \(c_1 = \frac{\vvec\cdot\vvec_1}{\vvec_1\cdot\vvec_1}\text{.}\)
    In the same way, \(c_2 = \frac{\vvec\cdot\vvec_2}{\vvec_2\cdot\vvec_2}\) and \(c_3 = \frac{\vvec\cdot\vvec_3}{\vvec_3\cdot\vvec_3}\)
  3. Check that the vectors are orthogonal by computing their dot products. Then \(\vvec = 2\vvec_1 - \vvec_2 + 2\vvec_3\) by computing the ratios of dot products.

  3. We find the characteristic polynomial of \(A\) to be
    \begin{equation*} \det(A-\lambda I) = \lambda^2-\frac52\lambda+\frac12 p\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which has roots
    \begin{equation*} \lambda=\frac 54 \pm \frac12\sqrt{\frac94-2p}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So long as \(\frac94 - 2p\geq 0\text{,}\) one root will be real and larger than \(1\text{.}\) We have a saddle when
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac 54 - \frac12\sqrt{\frac94-2p} \amp \lt 1 \\ \sqrt{\frac94-2p} \amp \gt \frac12 \\ p \amp \lt 1\text{.} \\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    This shows that we have a saddle whenever \(p\lt1\text{.}\)
  4. When \(p=0\text{,}\) we have a saddle. Notice that \(R\) will go extinct if the initial population of \(R\) is zero. As we increase \(p\text{,}\) we still have a saddle, but \(R\) will now go extinct when the initial \(S\) population is positive. Eventually, when \(p=1\text{,}\) we see that \(R\) will go extinct as long as \(R_0\gt S_0\text{.}\)
    As we increase the predation rate, the population of \(S\) is lowered by \(R\text{.}\) However, \(S\) is necessary to \(R\)â€™s survival. When the predation rate grows too large, there needs to be a sufficiently large initial \(S\) population to support both species.
